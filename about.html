Atmosphere

Composition of the atmosphere

The atmosphere is composed mostly of nitrogen and oxygen, with smaller amounts of other gases like carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone. By volume, the dry air contains roughly 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and 1% other gases. Carbon dioxide is a particularly important gas in the atmosphere because of its role in the greenhouse effect, which affects Earth's climate. Carbon dioxide concentrations have been increasing since the Industrial Revolution due to human activities like burning fossil fuels and deforestation. Methane, nitrous oxide, and other gases are also potent greenhouse gases, contributing to climate change.

Where is the ozone layer located?

According to the European Environment Agency, the ozone layer is located in the stratosphere, which is the second layer of Earth's atmosphere. The ozone layer is a protective layer that absorbs most of the sun's harmful ultraviolet radiation, preventing it from reaching the Earth's surface. The ozone layer is crucial for human health and the environment as it protects us from the harmful effects of UV radiation. Ozone is a trace gas in the atmosphere and is mostly concentrated in the upper layers of the stratosphere.

Layers of the Atmosphere

Troposhere & Stratosphere

The troposphere is the lowest layer and extends from the Earth's surface up to about 12 km (7.5 miles) at the poles and up to 17 km (11 miles) at the equator. This layer is where weather occurs and contains about 80% of the atmosphere's total mass.

The stratosphere is above the troposphere and extends up to about 50 km (31 miles) above the Earth's surface. This layer contains the ozone layer, which absorbs harmful ultraviolet radiation from the sun.

Mesosphere & Thermosphere

The mesosphere is above the stratosphere and extends up to about 85 km (53 miles) above the Earth's surface. This layer is the coldest in the atmosphere and meteors often burn up in this layer.

The thermosphere is above the mesosphere and extends up to about 600 km (372 miles) above the Earth's surface. This layer contains the ionosphere, which is ionized by solar radiation and is responsible for auroras.

Exosphere

The exosphere is the outermost layer and extends up to about 10,000 km (6,200 miles) above the Earth's surface. This layer is where the atmosphere gradually blends into space.
