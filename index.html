What is the ozone layer?

The ozone layer is a part of the Earth's stratosphere, which is the second layer of the atmosphere that consists of protective gases. The term 'stratosphere' comes from the fact that it is stratified or layered, and its temperature increases with elevation because ozone gases in the upper layers absorb intense ultraviolet radiation from the sun. Despite being a trace gas in the atmosphere, ozone plays a crucial role in protecting life on Earth by absorbing harmful radiation from the sun. The ozone layer acts as a shield that protects living organisms from excessive UV radiation, which can be harmful to life on Earth.

Importance of the Ozone Layer

The ozone layer plays a crucial role in shielding the Earth from harmful UV-B radiation from the sun, which can damage DNA and life. In the past, human activities caused significant damage to the ozone layer, leading to the formation of an ozone hole and increased UV-B radiation on the Earth's surface. The effects of ozone depletion and increased UV-B radiation have been more severe in the Southern Hemisphere due to factors such as colder temperatures and polar stratospheric clouds. The increase in UV-B radiation can have detrimental effects on life, such as an increased risk of skin cancer. However, the global effort to eliminate ozone-depleting substances has resulted in the recovery of the ozone layer, and millions of skin cancer cases have been prevented as a result.

Timeline

1974 - 1978

1974: Scientists Mario Molina and Sherwood Rowland publish a paper proposing that chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) could deplete the ozone layer.

1978: The United States bans the use of CFCs in aerosol sprays.

1981 - 1987

1981: The British Antarctic Survey announces the discovery of the "ozone hole" over Antarctica.

1985: Scientists confirm that the depletion of ozone over Antarctica is caused by CFCs and other ozone-depleting substances.

1987: The Montreal Protocol is signed, committing countries to phasing out the production of ozone-depleting substances.

1990s - 1992

1990s: The hole over Antarctica continues to grow, reaching a maximum size of 29.9 million square kilometers in 2006.

1992: The Copenhagen Amendment is added to the Montreal Protocol, accelerating the phase-out of ozone-depleting substances.

2000s - 2016

2000s: The ozone layer begins to show signs of recovery, and the hole over Antarctica begins to shrink.

2016: The Kigali Amendment to the Montreal Protocol is adopted, committing countries to phasing out hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), which are potent greenhouse gases and ozone-depleting substances.
